The Craft

How every piece
comes alive

01

Coating

Each garment is hand-coated with a photosensitive emulsion of ferric ammonium citrate and potassium ferricyanide — the same chemistry photographers used in the 1840s.

02

Exposure

Botanical specimens or hand-drawn acetate negatives are placed directly on the fabric and exposed to UV light. The sun does the printing. Every print is weather-dependent and unique.

03

Fixing & Finishing

The piece is washed, fixed with a hydrogen peroxide solution, and finished with a tannin treatment to maximise wash durability. Each piece is inspected, signed, and numbered.

Preservation Guide

Care for your
cyanotype

Cyanotype is a photochemical process — beautiful, archival, and honest about its nature. With the right care, your piece will age gracefully and hold its depth for years.

〜

Washing

Cold water only — 30°C maximum. Turn the garment inside out before every wash. Use a gentle, pH-neutral detergent. Hand wash preferred; if machine washing, use a delicate cycle. Never wring or twist the fabric — press gently to remove excess water.

☀

Drying

Dry in the shade, always. Prolonged UV exposure after washing can cause uneven fading. Lay flat or hang in a cool, shaded space. Never tumble dry — the heat degrades the iron compounds that give cyanotype its distinctive blue depth.

✕

What to Avoid

No bleach, ever. Bleach destroys the chemistry permanently — there is no recovery. Avoid fabric softeners, harsh detergents, and alkaline soaps. Do not iron directly over the printed area; iron on reverse only at low heat.

◇

Storage

Store folded in a cool, dark place. Long-term exposure to direct light between wears can subtly shift the tones. Keep away from damp areas. If storing for an extended period, wrap loosely in acid-free tissue.

!
Expected Lifespan With Proper Care With cold washing, shade drying, and no bleach — each Cyan Outlaw piece is treated to last 40 to 50 washes. After that, tones will gently fade and shift — a natural characteristic of the medium, not a defect. Many of our customers consider this ageing part of the piece's story.

Our Story

Made slow,
on purpose

Cyan Outlaw began as an experiment — what happens when you take a printing process from 1842 and put it on a t-shirt? The answer turned out to be something people wanted to wear.

Every piece we make is coated, exposed, and fixed by hand. We don't run batches. We don't have a factory. We have a studio, some chemistry, and an obsession with getting the blue exactly right.

The name came from the idea that making things slowly, by hand, in 2024 is a kind of rebellion. We're fine with that.
